Week Two

Interim Eval

Fig. 29: 1600 ISO handheld

The most likely reason you're reading this review is that you are considering purchasing the K20D and you want to figure out whether you should or no. So let's recap some fairly objective factoids:

  • Handling and features: standard modern 2 lb dSLR design with in-camera image stabilization, an excellent pentaprism finder, 2% spot metering, at least decent AF, 3 frames/sec, highly responsive, highly customizable.
  • Low ISO output: stunning 14.6 megapixel RAW images. Superb detail and colour plus reasonable, if not wonderful, DR, and low noise at ISOs up to 400. 800 ISO shows only the slightest degradation.
  • High ISO output: a bit on the noisy side; similar to last year's 10 megapixel dSLRs from 1600 to 6400 ISO.

The 14.6 mp number jumps out as the current lead in the semi-pro class. You can confirm for yourself that this is not just an empty number by looking at the full-size JPEGs available in this review. Many have theorized that the K10D's class-leading 10 mp RAW resolution might be attributed to a weak anti-aliasing filter; if so, this continues to be true for the K20D.

Not everyone needs that many megapixels, however superb. The K20D's at best average high ISO capabilities, combined with in-camera IS, are also a potent combination for handheld, low-light shooting. On the downside, the combination of unexceptional AF and lowish FPS make for a camera that fast-action shooters will want to take a pass on.

Technical confirmation

As I've stated repeatedly this is a pragmatic review. I may know something about how to take a photograph, but am not any kind of expert in engineering matters. It was therefore a great relief to me to have some confirmation that I haven't strayed too far from the course in the results I've reported so far. Shake reduction (AKA image stabilization)

IS testing probably requires some sort of calibrated vibration machine for consistent results. But just to get an idea of what to expect, I propped a res chart up on a desk top, stood several feet back then took successive frames at progressively slower shutters, once with SR off and once with SR on:

Fig. 30: IS testing

Clearly, SR is doing its job; but bear in mind I used the 16-45/4 at 45mm for this test (not having anything longer at present). No one expects to go as slow as 1/15th with significant tele glass, even with IS this good. The 1/60th with SR shot is every bit as sharp as a tripod shot with mirror lock-up. (BTW: although the manual says to turn SR off when shooting from a tripod, I haven't yet seen a difference .)

K10D vs K20D res comparision

About a year ago I took my tripod to my local camera store and shot some test pix with the K10D and the 16-45/f4. Today I went back to the same spot with the K20D and 16-45/f4 (and tripod) to try to re-create the same sequence. My purpose here is to provide a bit of objective evidence for those of you who own the K10D and feel tempted to upgrade. We already know that ISO and DR are not particularly different between the two cameras, leaving resolution and 12-bit vs 14-bit colour. Colour will have to wait for another day, since I need the same overcast sky of the K10D test to take a stab at that. However, we can get at least a preliminary feel for the difference in resolution. We know from the dpreview testing that the K10D was at the top of the 10 mp class in resolution. Here are crops from the same scene taken with the K10D and the K20D:

Fig. 31: K10D, 28mm @f/8 crop (Full size, level 12, 6.0 mp JPEG here for full size)

Fig. 32: K20D, 28mm @f/8 crop (Full size, level 12, 5.4 mp JPEG here for full size)

Click on the links to the full size JPEGs for un-edited JPEGs exported from Lightroom with no sharpening or other adjustments, to evaluate them for yourself. Ignore the contrast and colour between the two crops, since lighting conditions were not the same. For the above crops, I up-sampled the K10D file to the same size as the K20D file to make it easier to do visual comparison. I also sharpened both as best I could, although, as you can see, the K10D crop just doesn't want to come clean.

I don't think there's much question that the K20D's increase in fine detail is palpable. Where there is a question is which K10D owners will benefit from that increase. The above shots were taken on a tripod with mirror lock-up at f/8 (the sweet spot of most lenses), they contain subject matter such as distant bare branches and overhead wires that can benefit from the extra resolution. Given that all these conditions are met, then, yes: the K20D will allow you to make a bigger print than the K10D, or a more-finely detailed medium-size print. To me, this would seem to be of more concern to those who do landscape and group portraits then to those who do family events and sports, for example.
