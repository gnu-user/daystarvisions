The 16-bit TIFF file for this image, scanned at 4000 ppi, is 125 megabytes and 5776 by 3787 pixels, making it a very good match for printing on Epson's 13" x 19" paper size. Perhaps just a few small adjustments to make before it's ready for that, however... ;)

  • 7. After opening the scan file in Photoshop, crop away any black unexposed film edges you may have missed in Vuescan, then open the Levels dialogue:

Fig 6. Photoshop Levels before adjustment

  • 8. Push Levels sliders towards the edges of the pixel mountain range, then click OK.

Fig 7. Photoshop Levels after adjustment

Notice in Fig 7 that I've chosen to clip the stragglers of the dark end of the data but not the light end. This seems to work for most of my images. The important thing here is that you get to control the black and white points, not the scanner software. Is the world really so simple that every scene we view has a true black and a true white? Are you even ready to make this decision this early in the process?

  • 9. Check at 100% mag. whether noise (esp. sky and shadows) and scratches are acceptable:

Fig 8. Checking scan quality

Tips: In almost any dialogue or when using most editing tools you can hold down the spacebar to temporarily get the hand tool for panning (thanks, Andy!). You can also use Ctrl+PgUp and Ctrl+PgDn to pan left and right.

If the noise or the amount of scratching are too bad then you may want to return to Vuescan to apply a stronger level of IR cleaning and/or Grain Removal.
